### 1. Aortic Dissection

#### 1.1 Definition & Classification
Acute aortic dissection is defined as a tear in the aortic intima allowing blood to enter between the layers of the aortic wall, creating a false lumen and leading to propagation of the dissection. (NCBI)

The most common classification schema is the Stanford system
   Type A involves the ascending aorta (proximal to the brachiocephalic branch) — requires emergent surgical management. (NCBI)
   Type B involves the descending aorta (distal to the left subclavian artery) — often managed medically if uncomplicated. (StatPearls)

#### 1.2 Why It Matters
This is a life-threatening vascular emergency. Without treatment, the mortality is very high (≈ 50% within 48 h for ascending dissections). (Medscape)

Rapid recognition, diagnosis, and appropriate intervention (surgical or medical) are essential for survival. (Ahajournals)

#### 1.3 Clinical Presentation
Pain characteristics Often sudden onset, severe, described as “tearing,” “ripping” or “sharp,” located in the chest, back or between the shoulder blades, sometimes migrating as the dissection extends. (PubMed Central)

Associated symptoms Syncope, acute neurologic deficits (stroke-like), limb ischemia, visceralrenal ischemia, acute heart failure (if aortic regurgitation or tamponade), new murmur of aortic regurgitation. (NCBI)
